# Tutorial review criteria

## What a tutorial must do

A tutorial is a **lesson**. The reader is a beginner. They are trusting you to lead them somewhere safe and useful. At the end, they should have built or done something real, and they should understand more than they did before.

## Structure checklist

- [ ] Opens with what the reader will accomplish and learn (not just what they'll do)
- [ ] States clearly who it is for and what prior knowledge it assumes
- [ ] Leads the reader through a single, coherent narrative — no forks, no "if you want to..."
- [ ] Every step produces a visible or testable result
- [ ] Ends with a summary of what was learned and a clear pointer to next steps

## Content checklist

- [ ] All commands and code are complete and copy-pasteable
- [ ] Expected outputs are shown after each significant step
- [ ] Nothing is left to the reader to figure out — if they need to make a decision, the tutorial makes it for them
- [ ] Explanations are kept brief and tied to what's happening *right now* — deep theory goes in an Explanation doc, not here
- [ ] The learning arc is visible: the reader should feel themselves getting more capable as they progress

## Common tutorial failures

**Teaching by doing too much**: Piling in concepts unrelated to the learning goal. If it's not needed to complete the tutorial task, cut it or link to an Explanation.

**Leaving gaps**: Assuming the reader will "figure it out." Every tutorial gap is a support ticket. Fill them.

**Abstract opening**: Starting with "In this tutorial, you will learn about X" instead of "By the end of this tutorial, you will have built Y and you will understand Z."

**Branching paths**: "If you're on Windows, do A; if you're on Mac, do B." Tutorials should pick one path. Create separate tutorials if needed.

**No payoff**: The reader completes all the steps but doesn't have something tangible to show for it.
